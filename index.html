The C. elegans Connectome Predicts the Working Memory Circuit

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
C. elegans connectome hub neurons working memory neural dynamics Python

The Kato lab (Focolab) recently showed that short-term working memory in C. elegans is implemented by coupled oscillations between two distributed neural complexes — the backward command interneurons (AVA) and the head motor oscillators. The key finding from Dunn et al.: when the worm needs to hold a sensory memory before executing a turn, the phase relationship between these two oscillatory complexes encodes that memory.

What struck me reading this is that both AVA and AVB (forward command) were already known as network hubs from connectome topology — long before anyone did whole-brain imaging. I re-analyzed the C. elegans hermaphrodite connectome from the OpenWorm/c302 repository (White et al. 1986, 4,681 chemical synapses and 2,698 electrical gap junctions) to ask: does connectivity structure predict which neurons should coordinate global brain dynamics?

What this is. Personal exploratory re-analysis of publicly available data, not novel research. Hermaphrodite connectome edgelist from OpenWorm/c302 (White et al. 1986; Varshney et al. 2011 revision). Degree centrality = total weighted synapse count (chemical outgoing + incoming + electrical). Non-neuronal entities (hyp, mu_*) excluded.

AVA and AVB are the top network hubs — by a wide margin

AVAR and AVAL (backward command interneurons) are the top two hubs, with total weighted degrees of 1,429 and 1,390 — more than 50% above the next tier. AVA neurons receive dense chemical input from sensory and interneurons, project broadly to motor neurons, and are coupled to most of the network via gap junctions. This topology makes them natural coordinators of global state changes — exactly the role Dunn et al. assign them in the working memory circuit. The connectome structure was always pointing at AVA; it took whole-brain imaging to show how that connectivity produces oscillatory memory storage.
AVBL and AVBR (forward command interneurons) rank 7th and 8th despite being on the opposite side of the behavioral decision from AVA. In the working memory paper, AVA and AVB form the two poles of a coupled oscillatory system. The connectome shows why: both are deep in the hub tier, with overlapping but complementary connectivity profiles. AVA has more incoming chemical weight (660 vs. 338 for AVB), while AVB has a different gap junction pattern — structural asymmetries that likely underlie their distinct phase roles in the memory circuit.

AVA/AVB are chemical hubs; other neurons rely more on electrical coupling

RIA interneurons (RIAR, RIAL) stand out as pure chemical hubs with very little electrical coupling — in contrast to PVC and PVP neurons that combine moderate chemical with strong electrical connectivity. RIA neurons are sensorimotor integrators that modulate head direction during forward locomotion. The Kato lab's 2015 Cell paper (Global brain dynamics embed the motor command sequence of C. elegans) identified RIA as a key participant in the motor manifold. The purely chemical connectivity of RIA suggests it functions as a directed signal integrator, not a state synchronizer — which fits its role in translating motor commands into smooth head oscillation rather than coordinating global state switches.

Connection to the lab's work

  • Working memory paper (Dunn et al.): This analysis takes the specific neurons identified in the working memory circuit and asks what their structural position in the connectome predicts about their function. The answer — AVA is a chemical hub with massive incoming drive, AVB is a coupled hub with different balance — is consistent with their distinct oscillatory roles. Connecting structural and dynamical data is the kind of analysis I'd want to extend with the lab's whole-brain imaging datasets.
  • Hub neurons paper: The lab published on hub neurons and non-local connectivity features in global dynamics. This re-analysis is a simpler version of that question using only weighted degree — the next step would be to compute betweenness centrality and compare with the lab's more sophisticated network features, using the full imaging data to validate structural predictions.
  • Tardigrade systems neuroscience: The lab's push to establish tardigrades as a model organism is exciting from a comparative angle. I'd be curious to help develop analysis tools for tardigrade whole-brain data, particularly if it involves adapting the neuron identification and manifold analysis approaches the lab already has for C. elegans.
  • On the experimental side, I have hands-on experience with mammalian cell culture, fluorescence microscopy, flow cytometry, and RNA/protein-level assays. I'm comfortable contributing on both the bench and computational sides of the work.

Methods

Data downloaded July 2026 from OpenWorm/c302 (herm_full_edgelist.csv; White et al. 1986; Varshney et al. 2011). 4,681 chemical synapses and 2,698 electrical gap junctions in the adult hermaphrodite. Weighted degree = sum of all synapse weights (chemical outgoing + chemical incoming + electrical) per neuron. Non-neuronal targets (hyp, mu_*) excluded. Top 60 neurons by total degree shown in Figure 2. Neuron functional class annotations from WormAtlas and Kato lab publications. Figures: pure Python SVG. Code: fetch_data.py, make_figures.py. Raw data: connectivity.tsv, hub_neurons.tsv.
